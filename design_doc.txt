TRENCH — DESIGN DOCUMENT
=========================
A campaign-driven war simulator inspired by Total War and Ultimate General.
Set in historical 1700s–WW1 Europe. The player is a GENERAL, not a nation.

Last updated: 2026-04-25


===============================
VISION
===============================

The player takes on the role of a military general serving one of the great
powers of Europe. You don't control the nation — the crown gives you orders,
budgets, and campaigns based on how much they trust and value you.

The game sits on two layers:
  1. STRATEGIC LAYER — A geopolitical map of Europe with regions, nations,
     armies, and resources. Turn-based, weekly ticks.
  2. TACTICAL LAYER — Pitched battles (top-down, real-time, pausable).
     Can be watched or auto-resolved at the player's choice.

The world is dynamic. Other generals and kings make their own decisions.
Wars break out, campaigns succeed or fail, borders shift — all around you.
Your career rises or falls based on your performance within that living world.

The focus is on PITCHED BATTLES of the era — massed formations meeting on a
field — rather than the continuous trench fronts of WW1 (though we may get
there eventually, hence the name).


===============================
THE PLAYER — A GENERAL'S CAREER
===============================

You start as a low-ranking officer assigned a small command. Win battles,
gain recognition, and receive bigger assignments with more troops and more
important objectives. The crown's faith in you determines what you're given.

RECOGNITION (three-part reputation system):

  PRESTIGE — Military success. Driven by wins and losses, the scale of your
  victories, and strategic achievements. The core measure of competence.

  INFAMY — How "moral" you are. Determined by treatment of POWs, behavior
  in occupied territories, treatment of locals and civilian populations.
  High infamy makes you feared but may cost you allies and crown favor.

  NOTORIETY — The multiplier. How much attention the world pays to you.
  A famous general's victories (and defeats) echo louder. Every step matters
  more when everyone is watching.

GENERAL STATS:

  Loyalty     — Devotion to the crown. Loyal generals follow orders even when
                they're bad. Disloyal ones may refuse campaigns, defect, or
                worse if powerful enough.

  Happiness   — Personal satisfaction. Affected by pay, quality of commands,
                troop supply, and whether they feel valued. Unhappy generals
                perform worse or resign.

  Crown Opinion — How much the king trusts and favors you. Win battles and it
                rises. Become too famous and independent, it may drop. A king
                might sideline a brilliant general he doesn't trust.

  Tension: the public loves you (high notoriety + prestige) but the crown
  gets nervous about your popularity (crown opinion drops). This is literally
  the Napoleon story — and it can happen to the player.

All AI generals also have these stats. They make decisions based on personality,
ambition, loyalty, and self-interest.


===============================
TIME SYSTEM
===============================

The strategic layer runs on WEEKLY TICKS.

  SPRING (March–May) — Campaign season opens. Nations declare intentions,
  generals receive orders, armies muster and begin marching. Early campaigns
  try to strike before the enemy is ready.

  SUMMER (June–August) — Peak fighting season. Armies moving, battles
  happening, this is where the action is densest.

  FALL (September–November) — Late campaigns get risky. Racing against the
  calendar. Push too far and you're caught in winter far from supply. Smart
  generals consolidate gains.

  WINTER (December–February) — Armies in winter quarters. Reduced movement,
  no major campaigns (unless desperate or reckless — and it costs you).
  Nations recruit, build weapons, negotiate. The player reviews finances,
  replaces losses, plans for spring. Fast-forward option available.

A year is ~52 weekly ticks. A campaign might last 8–20 weeks depending on
scale and distance.


===============================
CAMPAIGN SYSTEM
===============================

A campaign is an attempt to control one or more REGIONS, defined before it
begins. The crown assigns a campaign to a general based on their recognition
and crown opinion.

Campaigns can vary in scale:
  - Small: seize an island, capture a port city
  - Medium: conquer a province, defend a border
  - Large: Napoleon-style invasion across multiple regions

The crown provides a BASE BUDGET proportional to the campaign's importance.
The general then manages that budget — deciding how to spend on recruitment,
equipment, and supply. You're not funding the invasion of Russia yourself,
but you decide whether to bring more infantry or more cannon.

Each battle in a campaign has consequences:
  - Winners hold/take ground, losers retreat
  - Casualties carry over — no magic refills between battles
  - Supply lines and distance from home territory matter
  - Army morale (distinct from unit morale) shifts with wins/losses

Campaign ends when:
  - Objectives are met (attacker wins)
  - Attacker is repelled or exhausted (defender wins)
  - Peace is negotiated

Player can choose to WATCH battles (tactical layer) or AUTO-RESOLVE them.


===============================
ARMY MANAGEMENT & RECRUITMENT
===============================

When assigned a campaign, the general receives:
  - A budget (gold) from the crown
  - Access to the nation's manpower pool
  - Access to the nation's weapon stockpiles

The general then builds their army:

  RECRUITMENT — Spend gold + manpower to raise units.
    - Infantry battalion: cheap, needs muskets
    - Cavalry squadron: moderate cost, needs horses
    - Artillery battery: expensive, needs cannons

  EQUIPMENT — Units need weapons from the stockpile.
    - Infantry: 1 musket per man (~100 per battalion)
    - Cavalry: horses + sabers (some with pistols/carbines)
    - Artillery: cannons (1-4 per battery) + ammunition

  TRADE-OFFS — More recruits vs better equipment. A large poorly-armed
  force vs a small elite one. This feeds directly into the battle sim —
  your pre-battle decisions determine what shows up on the field.

  UPKEEP — Armies cost gold and food per week while active.
  Underfunded armies suffer desertion and morale loss.


===============================
RESOURCES
===============================

Four core resources that NATIONS manage (player interacts through budget):

MANPOWER
  - Population-based; replenishes slowly over time
  - Recruiting troops depletes the pool
  - Casualties are gone (dead) or slowly recover (wounded)
  - Large wars drain nations — pyrrhic victories feel costly

MONEY / GOLD
  - Funds recruitment, equipment, upkeep, supply
  - Generated by trade, taxation, controlled regions
  - Wars are expensive; prolonged campaigns drain the treasury
  - Running out = attrition, desertion, inability to recruit

FOOD / SUPPLY
  - Armies need to eat; supply lines from home territory
  - Foraging possible but limited and harms local relations (infamy)
  - Armies far from supply take attrition
  - Sieges and blockades can cut supply to defenders

WEAPONS (Firearms, Cannons, Horses)
  - Produced over time; require money and industry
  - Infantry need muskets, cavalry need horses, artillery need cannons
  - Losing battles means losing equipment
  - Quality tiers later (smoothbore → rifled → breech-loading)


===============================
STRATEGIC MAP — HISTORICAL EUROPE
===============================

A simplified but recognizable map of Europe, divided into regions.

Regions:
  - Have terrain types (plains, mountains, coast, forest)
  - Produce resources (farmland=food, mines=gold, cities=manpower, industry=weapons)
  - Contain key locations: capitals, ports, forts, mountain passes
  - Control of a region = control of its resources

Starting nations (1700s era):
  - France — large, wealthy, strong army, central position
  - Great Britain — island, strong navy, colonial wealth
  - Austria — large but diverse, central European power
  - Prussia — small but disciplined military, efficient
  - Russia — vast manpower, harsh terrain, slow to mobilize
  - Spain — declining but still powerful, colonial holdings
  - Ottoman Empire — large, different military tradition
  (Possible additions: Sweden, Netherlands, Poland, minor states)

Each nation has:
  - Home territory (several regions)
  - National traits/bonuses (disciplined infantry, strong navy, etc.)
  - A pool of generals with varying stats and personalities
  - Diplomatic relations: alliance, neutral, rivalry, war
  - AI decision-making for wars, campaigns, and resource allocation


===============================
UNIT TYPES
===============================

Current (v1.1):
  - Line Infantry — backbone of any army, musket volleys, holds the line
  - Cavalry — fast, flanking, devastating charges, vulnerable to formed infantry
  - Artillery — long range, area damage, slow, vulnerable up close

Planned additions:
  - Light Infantry / Skirmishers — screen ahead of main line, harass, faster
  - Grenadiers / Elite Infantry — tougher, better morale, expensive
  - Dragoons — mounted infantry, can dismount to fight on foot
  - Engineers / Sappers — siege work, field fortifications, bridge building
  - Generals / Commanders — on-field presence, morale/ability bonuses

Later era units (as tech progresses):
  - Riflemen — longer range, more accurate, slower rate of fire
  - Machine guns — devastating defensive firepower
  - Early tanks — slow, terrifying, unreliable


===============================
BATTLE IMPROVEMENTS (NEAR TERM)
===============================

Things to add to the tactical battle layer:

- Variable army composition (fed from recruitment, not hardcoded)
- Unit facing and orientation (matters for flanking)
- Fatigue system (units tire over long engagements)
- Weather effects (rain reduces firearm effectiveness, fog limits range)
- Time of day (night battles, dawn attacks)
- Field fortifications (infantry can dig in for defense bonus)
- Commander abilities (rally broken troops, force march, concentrated volley)
- Multiple battle maps / terrain generation
- Reserves system (not all units deploy immediately)
- Supply/ammo — units can run out of ammunition
- Retreating armies vs routed armies (orderly withdrawal vs panicked flight)
- Auto-resolve option (calculate result without playing battle)


===============================
ARCHITECTURE
===============================

Single HTML file. One global game state object (like the hockey sim's G).

Core data structures:
  - G.nations[]     — all nations with resources, generals, diplomacy
  - G.regions[]     — map regions with ownership, terrain, production
  - G.generals[]    — all generals (player is one of these)
  - G.armies[]      — active armies on the map
  - G.campaigns[]   — active and completed campaigns
  - G.player        — reference to the player's general
  - G.week          — current week number
  - G.year          — current year
  - G.season        — spring/summer/fall/winter

Strategic turn flow (per week):
  1. Collect resources for each nation
  2. Pay army upkeep
  3. Check for new wars / peace negotiations
  4. AI assigns campaigns and generals
  5. Armies move one region toward objectives
  6. Check for battle triggers (armies in same region)
  7. Resolve battles (auto or tactical)
  8. Update recognition scores
  9. Check campaign victory conditions
  10. Advance week, check season transitions

UI views:
  - Strategic map (main view)
  - Army management / recruitment
  - General profile (stats, recognition, career history)
  - Campaign overview (objectives, progress, army status)
  - Battle view (existing tactical sim)
  - Nation overview (resources, wars, diplomacy)
  - Winter quarters (planning, recruitment, diplomacy)
